Das weiße Band - Eine deutsche Kindergeschichte, or, The White Ribbon directed by Michael Haneke Brief Overview (no spoilers): In a small German town a few years before World War I, numerous bizarre and unexplained events occur, affecting the adolescence and growth of the younger generation in the town. Verdict: Back to one of the GOATs with my first Haneke review in a while (I watched Cache semi-recently, but unsure when either review will get posted). Before I get into the review I want to point out something I learned recently which is not only cool, but also shows the intellect of Haneke in a way that I don't think I've seen before: The original title is Das weiße Band, which translates to The White Ribbon, but there is a subtitle (Eine deutsche Kindergeschichte) which is left untranslated (it translates to A German Children's Story). The film is about the roots of evil: radicalism/extremism/communal guilt in any form, but the children in the movie are a part of the German generation that would become Nazis. Haneke left the subtitle untranslated so that German audiences could read it and would regard the film as discussing the roots and growth of fascism and Nazism, while non-German audiences couldn't read it, and could then interpret the film as a discussion of whatever form of radicalism/extremism they know and relate to, not specifically fascism. That is just awesome. As for the film itself, it is pretty darn good. Not my personal favorite of his, but probably the most culturally impactful and important. I actually can't think of another movie that I have seen that discusses and shows potential roots and subsequent growth of these sorts of radical ideas without ever actually showing any part of the war or the aftermath, etc. I think it makes it that much more powerful because we KNOW what comes next, and we KNOW what these kids will grow into. This shows us how some of them might have ended up like that, rather than show the same sorts of things we typically see with Nazi-related movies. The pacing is generally good, though a couple scenes felt a bit too long. I like how the narration has so much info that is technically unimportant (names, exact ages, etc.), but it adds so much to the realism and your ability to picture this as a true story. I wish we had a couple more scenes with Martin and Klara. This is just such a dark movie in so many ways. It really does feel like an exploration and a documentation of the roots of evil and the many forms it can take. Haneke's accolade list is long, and man does he deserve every single one of them. Code Unknown and Amour are the big two I have left... My Thoughts (SPOILERS!!): No spoilers today! Watch the movie! What do you guys think? Discuss in the comments below!

The Worst Person in the World directed by Joachim Trier Brief Overview (no spoilers): The finale of Trier's "Oslo Trilogy" (surrounding the exploration of social exclusion) follows multiple tumultuous years in the life of Julie, a young woman struggling to navigate her work and personal life. Verdict: This is easily one of the best movies I've seen in a long long time, possibly ever. Trier takes two of the hardest genres to nail (the coming-of-age and the romcom), and nails them so hard the hammer breaks. I saw that he described this as "a romcom for the people who hate romcoms". In terms of the movie itself, pretty much every "category" is knocked out of the park. The acting is phenomenal, the soundtrack is perfect, the chapter format is awesome, the darker corners it explores fit perfectly and realistically with the character and the story, the editing and visual effects are great, and so on!! As for the messaging, I can't remember the last film that nailed so many relevant topics and themes, as well as the main character herself. You know a character is well written when the Letterboxd girlies somehow take pride in being like/wanting to be like Julie, the immature guys slam her for making her own choices and thoughts about her body, and the rest of us see her for the sociopathic narcissist she is. I 100% guarantee that at some point during this movie it will feel like Julie is you, and that a specific line or scene or shot was written and made solely for you. This should be required viewing for all, especially those in their 20's and 30's. A brilliant story and journey matched by equally brilliant social commentary. And yes, the actress at the end is Emma from Skam. My Thoughts (SPOILERS!!): Many of my exact thoughts are written much more eloquently than I could have in a wonderful article in Senses of Cinema by Salvador Carrasco. I will probably paraphrase or direct quote him many times in this section. First off, I just want to say it's scary that Julie has become, as Salvador writes: "somewhat of an icon for existentially confused Millennials". Yes, she appears to change at the end, and that is fantastic, and that personality and mentality change is absolutely something that should be applauded, but that's not the part that the gals seem to idolize… they'd rather salute their queen who literally cheats like 25 minutes into the movie and then doubles down a few minutes later, who is a sociopath, who is narcissistic beyond belief, and so on. The reporter's line: "But you're using your male privilege to mock people weaker than you. It's hardly art or even humor.", is a great little line because, of course, that is exactly what Trier is doing making this film (to universal acclaim from all genders I must add). 10/10 What do you guys think?
